Introduction

Colour Composite Images

From one single band of satellite data, the difference in energy levels between various land cover classifications may not be distinct. Since comparing the spectral characteristics of land features in multiple bands provides a better separation, or contrast, between different land surfaces, multiple bands of satellite data can be combined to create a data product known as a composite image. The composite images are often called three-band composite images since they are created using the measured energy level in each of three spectral bands to control the amount of red, blue, and green in a color output image.

Theory

True-Color Composite (321)

A true-color composite uses following channels to create an image
Band 3 (Visible red) = red
Band 2 (Visible green) = green
Band 1 (Visible blue-green) = blue
True-color images are often low in contrast and hazy in appearance since blue light is more easily scattered by the atmosphere.


Near Infrared Composite (432)

Band 4 (NIR) = red
Band 3 (Visible red) = green
Band 2 (Visible green) = blue
A 432 composite very useful in determining the extent of vegetation and in classifying different vegetation types

Short-wave Infrared Composite (743 or 742)

A Short-wave Infrared composite contains at least one band in the short-wave infrared (SWIR) portion of the electromagnetic spectrum
Band 7 (SWIR) = red
Band 4 (NIR) = green
Band 3 (Visible red) = blue

Band 7 (SWIR) = red
Band 4 (NIR) = green
Band 2 (Visible green) = blue
SWIR composite image is used for detecting vegetation stress.
Very useful in detecting soil types and soil disturbance since moisture is an important characteristic of soil structure
